Storm Response

What to do after storm damage

Storms can turn a safe dock and lift setup into a risky one quickly. The right first steps help protect people, prevent added damage, and speed up repair decisions.

After a major storm, the first priority is safety. Fallen debris, shifted dock sections, damaged lifts, unstable shorelines, and electrical issues can all create hazards. Avoid the instinct to start moving equipment immediately unless you know the area is safe.

First steps after the storm passes

  1. Stay clear of obvious hazards. Watch for unstable dock sections, damaged power equipment, or washed-out shoreline areas.
  2. Document the scene. Take wide and close photos before anything is moved. This helps with service planning and insurance conversations.
  3. Check the shoreline approach. A storm may have changed bank stability or access to the dock area.
  4. Do not force damaged equipment. If a lift or dock component looks twisted, jammed, or displaced, forcing it can make the damage worse.

What to look for

  • Shifted or partially disconnected dock sections
  • Lift frames that are leaning, twisted, or out of level
  • Canopy or accessory damage
  • Debris impact along the shoreline or around the lift
  • Erosion, washout, or undermined access points

How to speed up help

When you reach out for service, include the lake name, property location, a short description of what changed, and photos from multiple angles. That helps a service team prioritize the response and decide whether repair, removal, stabilization, or deeper shoreline support is needed.

When damage may be more serious than it looks

Some storm damage is structural, not just cosmetic. Equipment that still “works” may still be unsafe or poorly aligned. Shoreline impacts can also create delayed problems that show up only after the next heavy rain or wave event.

The goal after a storm

The goal is not only cleanup. It is returning the waterfront to a safe, reliable condition and preventing a one-time storm from becoming a longer-term property problem.
